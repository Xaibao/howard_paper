Motor Oil (Used Engine Oil) Contamination
==========================================

Chemical Composition:
Motor oil (used engine oil) is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons (mineral oil base), additives (detergents, antioxidants, anti-wear agents), and combustion byproducts (heavy metals like lead, zinc, cadmium, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons - PAHs).

Spectral Characteristics (300-1000 nm):
- Motor oil has a distinctly DIFFERENT spectral profile from food oils (correlation ~0.6-0.78 with others)
- Strong absorption in the 400-600 nm range due to aromatic hydrocarbons and degradation products
- Dark brown/black color causes high absorption across visible spectrum
- Characteristic lower reflectance compared to clear vegetable oils
- Easiest to distinguish among FOG classes due to mineral vs. biological origin

Pollution Sources and Origins:
- Automotive repair shops and garages
- Industrial machinery lubricant discharge
- Illegal dumping of used engine oil
- Storm drain contamination from parking lots
- Marine vessel engine discharge
- Railroad track maintenance areas

Environmental Hazards:
- Extremely persistent in environment (does not biodegrade easily)
- Contains carcinogenic PAHs (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons)
- 1 liter of motor oil can contaminate up to 1,000,000 liters of water
- Heavy metals (Pb, Zn, Cd) cause long-term soil and water toxicity
- Bioaccumulation in aquatic organisms
- Classified as hazardous waste in most countries

Immediate Identification Indicators:
- Dark coloration (dark brown to black)
- Characteristic petroleum/hydrocarbon odor
- Rainbow sheen on water surface (thin film interference)
- Very viscous texture
- Does not dissolve in water (forms floating layer)

Emergency Response Protocol:
1. Immediate containment: Deploy floating oil booms around contamination zone
2. Source identification: Trace upstream to find discharge point
3. Mechanical removal: Use oil skimmers and vacuum trucks
4. Report to environmental authorities (criminal offense in most jurisdictions)
5. Monitor for 30 days post-cleanup for groundwater impact

Treatment Methods:
- Mechanical: Skimming, boom containment, vacuum extraction
- Chemical: Dispersants (controversial - toxic to marine life)
- Biological: Bioremediation using oil-degrading bacteria (Alcanivorax, Marinobacter)
- Physical: Activated carbon adsorption for trace contamination
- In-situ burning (only for large offshore spills)

Regulatory Limits:
- WHO guideline: <0.01 mg/L total petroleum hydrocarbons in drinking water
- EPA limit: 0.1 mg/L in surface water discharge
- Immediate shutdown required if detected at industrial discharge points
